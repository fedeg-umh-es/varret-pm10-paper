We evaluate multi-horizon daily PM$_{10}$ point forecasts under a rolling-origin
protocol with persistence as an explicit baseline, comparing a histogram-based
gradient-boosted regressor (HGB direct), ridge regression (Ridge direct), and
seasonal naive across 17 monitoring stations from the Spanish MITECO national
air-quality network and forecast horizons $h \in \{1,\ldots,7\}$ days.
Direct ML models (HGB, Ridge) achieve moderate positive persistence-relative
RMSE skill (0.05--0.25 across horizons and stations) yet collapse predictive
variance: the variance-retention ratio $\alpha = \mathrm{Var}(\hat{y})/\mathrm{Var}(y)$
falls below the 0.5 collapse threshold at every station and every horizon, with
100\% of the 238 evaluated model--station--horizon cells flagged as collapsed.
Seasonal naive serves as a positive control: it preserves observed variance
almost perfectly ($\alpha \approx 0.986$, 0\% collapse) while correctly
confirming that the diagnostic discriminates between models with and without
dynamic fidelity.
Bootstrap confidence intervals on $\alpha$ ($n=1\,000$, 95\%) confirm that the
collapse of direct ML models is statistically robust across all 17 stations.
The overall collapse rate across all three models is 66.7\% (238/357 cells).
We introduce \mbox{Skill$_{VP}$} as a diagnostic auxiliary that adjusts
persistence-relative RMSE skill by variance retention, making the
skill--fidelity decoupling explicit at each horizon.
The results support reporting variance retention alongside RMSE-based skill as
a practical complement for horizon-wise interpretation under rolling-origin
evaluation.

Replace the entire Section 3.4 "Empirical models: LightGBM and SARIMA" with:

\subsection{Empirical models}

We report results for three point-forecasting models, all trained and evaluated
under the same rolling-origin protocol.

The \textbf{HGB direct} model is a HistGradientBoostingRegressor
(max\_iter=100, learning\_rate=0.05, max\_leaf\_nodes=15, random\_state=42),
a modern gradient-boosted decision tree well-suited to tabular regression.
It is fitted independently for each horizon $h$ using seven lag features
$\ell_0,\ldots,\ell_6$ (lag-0 is the observation at the forecast origin).
No meteorological inputs are used.

The \textbf{Ridge direct} model applies ridge regression ($\lambda=1$) preceded
by standard feature scaling (zero mean, unit variance), fitted on the same
seven lag features and the same direct multi-horizon strategy.

The \textbf{Seasonal naive} model predicts
$\hat{y}_{t+h\mid t} = y_{t+h-7}$, i.e., the observed value from seven days
prior to the target date.
It is included as a \emph{positive control}: by construction it mirrors the
weekly seasonal cycle and is expected to preserve near-unity variance retention
regardless of its RMSE skill, thereby providing a reference against which the
variance collapse of the direct ML models can be evaluated.

% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}

The evidence in this manuscript establishes a consistent and statistically
robust pattern across 17 geographically and typologically diverse MITECO
stations: direct ML point forecasters trained under squared-error criteria on
lag features collapse their predictive variance below the diagnostic threshold
($\alpha < 0.5$) at every horizon and station, despite achieving moderate
positive persistence-relative RMSE skill at most horizons.
Seasonal naive confirms that the diagnostic correctly discriminates: a model
that predicts from the lagged observation seven days prior preserves near-unity
variance ($\alpha \approx 0.986$) because its predictions inherit the amplitude
of the original series.

The contrast is sharpest at $h=1$, where HGB and Ridge already achieve their
lowest $\alpha$ values while still outperforming persistence on RMSE, and at
$h=7$, where Ridge $\alpha$ approaches 0.06 -- predictions that are nearly
constant.
The monotonic decrease of $\alpha$ with horizon for both direct ML models is
consistent with the expected behaviour of regressor-to-mean shrinkage under
squared-error loss: the conditional mean becomes an increasingly smooth function
of distant lags as predictability diminishes.

Skill$_{VP}$ is useful because it collapses the two-dimensional skill--retention
diagnostic into a single horizon-wise number.
For HGB and Ridge, Skill$_{VP}$ is near zero at all horizons despite RMSE skill
of 0.19--0.25, making the skill--fidelity decoupling visible in a single
summary.
For seasonal naive, Skill$_{VP}$ is also near zero but for the opposite reason:
positive variance retention with near-zero RMSE skill.
Figure~\ref{fig:scatter_skill_alpha} places the two failure modes in clearly
separated regions of the skill--$\alpha$ plane.

The consistency of the collapse finding across industrial, urban, and rural
stations supports the view that this is a systematic property of the
lag-feature direct-horizon paradigm under daily PM$_{10}$ dynamics, not a
station-specific artefact.
Bootstrap CIs confirm statistical robustness at every site.
The operational implication is that RMSE skill alone is insufficient evidence
of forecast usefulness in this setting: models that score well on RMSE while
collapsing variance are effectively predicting a smooth central tendency and
will miss pollution episodes.

% ============================================================
\section{Limitations}

This study is intentionally limited in scope.
The empirical evidence covers 17 MITECO stations at the daily temporal scale;
transfer to other pollutants, temporal resolutions, or national networks is
not assessed.
The results are restricted to lag-feature direct-horizon models (HGB direct,
Ridge direct) and seasonal naive; models with explicit seasonal decomposition,
meteorological inputs, or probabilistic output may exhibit different
variance-retention behaviour.
Skill$_{VP}$ is not proposed as a general-purpose replacement for RMSE or as a
new standard verification score; it is a simple diagnostic auxiliary whose value
lies in making the skill--fidelity decoupling explicit at each horizon.
The collapse threshold $\alpha = 0.5$ is operationally motivated but not derived
from a formal loss criterion.

\section{Conclusion}

Across 17 Spanish MITECO monitoring stations evaluated under daily rolling-origin
protocols with persistence as an explicit baseline, direct ML forecasters
(HGB direct, Ridge direct) collapse predictive variance at every station and
horizon ($\alpha < 0.5$, 100\% of model--station--horizon cells) despite
achieving moderate positive RMSE skill.
Seasonal naive, included as a positive control, preserves near-unity variance
retention ($\alpha \approx 0.986$) while confirming that the diagnostic
correctly distinguishes models with and without dynamic fidelity.
The overall collapse rate across all three models is 66.7\% (238/357 cells);
bootstrap confidence intervals confirm statistical robustness at all stations.
Reporting variance retention alongside RMSE skill, and using Skill$_{VP}$ as a
diagnostic auxiliary, provides a practical way to make the skill--fidelity
decoupling explicit for horizon-wise interpretation, without requiring additional
probabilistic infrastructure or implying a universal replacement for established
verification practices.
