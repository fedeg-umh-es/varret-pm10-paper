\begin{abstract}
Verification of deterministic air-quality forecasts relies heavily on persistence-relative RMSE skill, yet a forecast can be certified as skilful while its trajectory is dynamically over-smoothed. We introduce variance retention as a complementary verification diagnostic and quantify this skill--fidelity tension in a rolling-origin multi-horizon evaluation ($h=1,\ldots,7$) over 17 heterogeneous stations from the Spanish MITECO monitoring network, using persistence as an explicit baseline.

Five model families are compared: two direct lag-only machine-learning forecasters (HGB direct and Ridge direct), a classical statistical baseline (SARIMA), a variance-preserving reference (seasonal naive), and a decomposition-based control (STL+Ridge). Direct HGB and Ridge achieve positive RMSE skill in all station--horizon cells and are frequently significantly better than persistence under BH-adjusted Diebold--Mariano tests, yet they exhibit near-universal variance collapse ($\alpha<0.5$) in 118/119 cells each. SARIMA partially reproduces the same pattern, collapsing in 110/119 cells. Seasonal naive preserves variance but does not improve RMSE on median, whereas STL+Ridge preserves or inflates variance and attains high exceedance recall at the cost of poor RMSE skill and many false alarms.

The results show that maximising RMSE skill alone is insufficient for operational use: performance must be diagnosed jointly in terms of baseline-relative accuracy, dynamic fidelity (variance retention), and event-detection behaviour.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Significance Statement}
Air-quality services increasingly rely on automatic PM$_{10}$ forecasts, which
are usually judged by whether they beat a simple persistence forecast on average
error. We show that this common test can mislead: across 17 Spanish monitoring
stations and five forecasting methods, models that clearly beat persistence on
average nonetheless flatten the day-to-day swings that matter for detecting
pollution episodes. We propose a simple complementary check---variance
retention---that flags this behaviour and separates genuinely informative
forecasts from over-smoothed ones, and we confirm the operational consequence
through exceedance detection. Reporting variance retention alongside standard
skill scores would make routine air-quality forecast verification more
trustworthy for episode-sensitive decisions.

\section{Introduction}

Daily PM$_{10}$ forecasts support a short-horizon interpretation in air-quality management and public-health communication, where decisions are sensitive to near-term changes as well as average error \cite{who2021,wilks2011}. In operational settings, however, usefulness depends not only on average error but also on whether forecasts reproduce meaningful day-to-day changes and identify episodes.

In multi-horizon settings, persistence-relative RMSE skill is a necessary summary because it tests whether a model improves over an explicit operational baseline \cite{hyndman2006}. However, point-forecast accuracy summaries remain incomplete for decision support \cite{gneiting2011}: a model can be significantly skilful in RMSE while producing trajectories that are dynamically over-smoothed.

To address this gap, we introduce variance retention as a post-evaluation diagnostic layer to read dynamic fidelity alongside RMSE-based skill in a rolling-origin daily PM$_{10}$ study. We complement the joint skill–fidelity reading with (i) bootstrap intervals for the variance-retention ratio $\alpha$, (ii) threshold-sensitivity checks for the collapse convention, (iii) BH-adjusted Diebold–Mariano tests against persistence, (iv) exceedance diagnostics for episode sensitivity and false alarms, and (v) a Murphy decomposition to connect the empirical patterns to forecast verification theory \cite{murphy1988}.

We further ask whether the skill–fidelity tension is specific to direct lag-only ML forecasters or also appears in classical statistical and decomposition-based alternatives.

\paragraph{Contributions.} This paper contributes to forecast-verification
practice, not to forecasting methodology or atmospheric-process understanding.
Specifically: (i) we formalise \emph{variance retention} $\alpha(h)$ as a
bounded, post-hoc verification diagnostic that complements persistence-relative
skill by exposing amplitude collapse in deterministic point forecasts, together
with an interpretive skill--retention template (Table~\ref{tab:interpretive});
(ii) across 17 heterogeneous PM$_{10}$ stations and five model families, we show
that statistically significant RMSE gains over persistence (BH-adjusted
Diebold--Mariano) routinely coexist with near-universal variance collapse and
degraded event detection, and we connect this pattern to a Murphy decomposition
of mean squared error; and (iii) we distil a minimum set of reporting practices
for verifying deterministic air-quality forecasts. The claims are deliberately
bounded to the evaluated setting and are not offered as a general theory of
forecast skill.

\section{Evaluation Reporting Gaps in Particulate Matter Forecasting}

Machine-learning and deep-learning air-quality forecasting has grown rapidly, especially for particulate matter. Reviews describe increasingly complex architectures, hybrid modelling strategies, and data-driven pipelines for PM$_{10}$ and PM$_{2.5}$ prediction \cite{mendez2023,tang2024,chadalavada2025}.

This growth has shifted much of the literature toward comparisons between modelling architectures, feature sets, and learning algorithms. However, methodological claims about ``predictive gains'' are only interpretable when the evaluation design is stated clearly enough that a reader can understand what was tested, what the baseline comparator was, and how the forecast horizons were handled \cite{makridakis2018,cerqueira2020}.

Five evaluation reporting dimensions are central to operational interpretability in particulate matter forecasting: temporal validation, leakage-safe preprocessing, persistence or naive baselines, horizon-wise reporting, and operational-usefulness framing. Temporal validation establishes deployment-like chronological credibility; leakage-safe preprocessing protects against information bleed across train--test boundaries; persistence baselines provide a minimum operational comparator; horizon-wise reporting supports multi-step interpretability; and operational framing links performance summaries to decision relevance \cite{tashman2000,kaufman2012,jolliffe2012}.

To motivate why an additional trajectory-fidelity diagnostic can be useful in practice, we conducted a PRISMA~2020 audit of PM$_{10}$/PM$_{2.5}$ forecasting studies (Scopus and Web of Science, 2015--2025; reproducible materials archived at Zenodo, concept DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18675211, release: 10.5281/zenodo.19125915). At the abstract level, key evaluation choices (e.g., explicit persistence baselines, horizon-wise reporting, and temporally appropriate validation) were often not made visible. These rates reflect reporting opacity rather than confirmed absence. The need for such a diagnostic is amplified by a broader reporting problem in PM forecasting evaluation: when evaluation details and trajectory behaviour are not reported, baseline-relative improvements can be difficult to interpret operationally.



\section{Post-Evaluation Diagnostic Framework}

The post-evaluation framework is organised around three complementary questions:

\textbf{Skill:} Does the model beat persistence? In this manuscript, skill is defined relative to persistence under RMSE, so positive \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)\) indicates lower RMSE than persistence at the same horizon \(h\) \cite{hyndman2006,tashman2000}.

\textbf{Variance retention:} Does the forecast retain observed variability? The variance-retention ratio \(\alpha(h)=\mathrm{SD}(\hat{y})/\mathrm{SD}(y)\) is interpreted post-evaluation; low \(\alpha(h)\) indicates variability shrinkage consistent with smoothed trajectories, which can complicate operational interpretation even when RMSE skill is positive.

\textbf{Skill$_{VP}$:} What compact auxiliary summary helps read both together? Skill$_{VP}$ is reported only as a bounded diagnostic auxiliary alongside \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)\) and \(\alpha(h)\), not as a replacement for standard accuracy metrics.

\section{Case Study and Experimental Setting}

The case study uses daily PM$_{10}$ observations from 17 monitoring stations in the Spanish MITECO national air-quality network. The station set is intentionally heterogeneous in site type (industrial, traffic, urban/background, residential, and rural/remote), spans multiple autonomous communities (including the Comunitat Valenciana, Catalunya, Arag\'on, and Castilla-La Mancha), and covers the 2017--2024 period, with observational coverage ranging from 80.4\% to 98.5\%.

Three stations are described in detail as representative examples of the three environmental types.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Elx-Agroalimentari} (ES0446A): suburban/industrial station; 44\,m a.s.l.; 2,350 observed daily values (80.4\% coverage, 2017--2024).
  \item \textbf{Valencia-Vivers} (ES1619A): urban/residential station; 11\,m a.s.l.; 2,679 observed daily values (91.7\% coverage, 2017--2024).
  \item \textbf{Zarra-EMEP} (ES0012R): rural remote EMEP station; 855\,m a.s.l.; 2,804 observed daily values (96.0\% coverage, 2017--2024).
\end{itemize}

Meteorological covariates (temperature, relative humidity, wind speed,
and wind direction) are available for Elx-Agroalimentari only but are
deliberately excluded from all experiments: the study intentionally employs a lags-only design to isolate the autoregressive signal.

Each series was reindexed to a complete daily calendar before lag
construction. Dates with missing PM$_{10}$ values, missing lagged
predictors, or unavailable verification targets after horizon alignment
were excluded by listwise deletion. No interpolation or imputation was
applied.

The objective is not to maximise predictive accuracy, but to create a transparent evaluation setting in which persistence-relative skill, horizon-wise behaviour, and variance retention can be inspected jointly.

Five diagnostic comparators are evaluated. HGB direct and Ridge direct are direct lag-feature ML forecasters. SARIMA is included as a sparse classical statistical baseline. STL+Ridge is a decomposition-based variant used to test whether explicit seasonal separation can preserve trajectory variability. Seasonal naive is included as a variance-preserving seasonal reference. These models are not used as a leaderboard of optimised forecasting systems, but as diagnostic contrasts for separating baseline-relative accuracy from trajectory fidelity.

Forecasts are evaluated for horizons \(h=1,\dots,7\) using rolling-origin (time-series) evaluation with a mandatory persistence baseline, and the analysis focuses on point forecasts. When preprocessing is applicable (e.g., scaling), transformations are fitted on the training window only and then applied to the corresponding test window to reduce leakage risk \cite{bergmeir2018,kaufman2012}.

A lags-only design is intentionally restrictive: if positive skill appears even in this controlled setting, the variance-retention diagnostic can reveal whether the improvement is accompanied by retained forecast variability or by systematically smoothed trajectories.

\section{Metrics and Diagnostic Categories}

\subsection{Persistence-relative skill}
Let \(y_h\) denote the verifying observation at horizon \(h\), and \(\hat{y}_h\) the corresponding point forecast produced by a model. Let \(\hat{y}^{(p)}_h\) denote the persistence baseline forecast for the same horizon. For a chosen loss function \(L(\cdot,\cdot)\), the baseline-relative (persistence-relative) skill score at horizon \(h\) is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Skill}(h) = 1 - \frac{\mathbb{E}[L(y_h,\hat{y}_h)]}{\mathbb{E}[L(y_h,\hat{y}^{(p)}_h)]}.
\end{equation}
In this work, the empirical results use an RMSE-based loss, so \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)>0\) indicates a lower RMSE than persistence for the same horizon and evaluation sample \cite{hyndman2006}.

Persistence-relative skill is useful because it anchors interpretation to an operationally meaningful comparator rather than to a model-to-model ranking alone. It also supports horizon-wise reading: in multi-step settings, skill can vary substantially across lead times.

At the same time, skill is a summary of pointwise error behaviour under a chosen loss. It does not, by itself, describe the trajectory shape of the point forecasts, and it does not indicate whether an apparent improvement is associated with reduced dynamic variability.

\subsection{Variance retention}
We define the variance-retention ratio at horizon \(h\) as
\begin{equation}
\alpha(h) = \frac{\mathrm{SD}(\hat{y}_h)}{\mathrm{SD}(y_h)}.
\end{equation}

The ratio \(\alpha(h)\) compares forecast trajectory variability with observed variability at the same horizon. Values \(\alpha(h)\ll 1\) indicate variance shrinkage consistent with smoothed point-forecast trajectories, while values \(\alpha(h)\gg 1\) indicate forecast variance inflation.

Variance retention should not be interpreted as a complete measure of trajectory quality. A forecast may retain the observed variance and still be biased, phase-shifted, or poor at reproducing individual episodes. The diagnostic is therefore intended to detect one specific failure mode—variance shrinkage—rather than to certify full dynamic adequacy.

Variance retention is not a calibration measure and is not intended to assess probabilistic uncertainty. It is a bounded post-evaluation diagnostic for reading point forecasts when operational interpretation depends on day-to-day fluctuations as well as on average accuracy \cite{jolliffe2012}.

\subsection{Auxiliary \texorpdfstring{\(\mathrm{Skill}_{VP}\)}{Skill\_VP}}
To summarise the joint behaviour of baseline-relative accuracy and variance retention, we also report an auxiliary variance-penalised skill diagnostic
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Skill}_{VP}(h) = \mathrm{Skill}(h)\cdot\alpha(h).
\end{equation}

The auxiliary diagnostic is intended to highlight cases where positive persistence-relative skill co-occurs with strong variance shrinkage. The product form is not claimed to be uniquely optimal; it is used here only as a transparent descriptive penalty that keeps the sign of \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)\) while reducing positive skill when forecast variance is strongly collapsed. It should not be interpreted as a replacement for standard accuracy metrics, uncertainty quantification, or statistical significance testing \cite{diebold1995}.

Accordingly, \(\mathrm{Skill}_{VP}(h)\) is used only as a bounded diagnostic layer for post-evaluation interpretation. Its role is to provide a compact joint summary alongside \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)\) and \(\alpha(h)\), rather than to define a universal performance criterion.

\subsection{Diagnostic flags}
In addition to \(\alpha(h)\) and \(\mathrm{Skill}_{VP}(h)\), the post-evaluation reports diagnose flags for horizon-wise interpretation. The \texttt{collapse\_flag} is used when \(\alpha(h) < 0.5\). The \texttt{inflation\_flag} is used when \(\alpha(h) > 1.5\). The \texttt{near\_ideal\_flag} is used when \(\mathrm{Skill}(h)>0\) and \(0.8 \leq \alpha(h) \leq 1.2\). The \texttt{low\_sample\_flag} indicates fewer than 30 verification samples for a model/horizon cell.

These thresholds are diagnostic conventions for this post-evaluation, not universal decision rules. A collapse flag does not invalidate a model, and an inflation flag does not imply a better dynamic representation; the flags are intended to make horizon-wise interpretation auditable and reproducible.

\section{Reproducible Workflow}

The five-model evaluation produces a unified station$\times$model$\times$horizon table with 595 rows (17 stations $\times$ 5 models $\times$ 7 horizons) containing persistence-relative RMSE skill, variance retention $\alpha$, Skill$_{VP}$, and bootstrap confidence intervals (\texttt{alpha\_ci\_low}, \texttt{alpha\_ci\_high}). Repository scripts regenerate all manuscript tables and figures from the underlying forecast outputs, enabling inspection of post-evaluation assumptions (horizon structure, baseline definition, and preprocessing boundaries) and reproducible recomputation of the diagnostic layer \cite{sandve2013}.

Reproducibility here is intended to support the auditability of the evaluation workflow rather than introduce a new forecasting architecture: readers can re-run the study and examine how persistence-relative skill, horizon-wise behaviour, and variance retention interact.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Five-model diagnostic summary}
\label{sec:results_five_model_summary}

Across 17 stations and seven forecast horizons, direct HGB and Ridge achieve positive RMSE skill in all station-horizon cells but show near-universal variance collapse. HGB collapses in 118/119 cells (99.2\%) and Ridge in 118/119 cells (99.2\%), with only two $h=1$ point-estimate exceptions: Huesca for HGB ($\alpha = 0.544$) and Barcelona Vall d'Hebron for Ridge ($\alpha = 0.502$). SARIMA exhibits a similar but slightly less extreme pattern, collapsing in 110/119 cells (92.4\%). Seasonal naive preserves variance (0/119 collapsed cells) but has slightly negative median skill, whereas STL+Ridge avoids variance collapse but inflates variability and performs substantially worse in RMSE skill. Thus, the observed trade-off is not a simple model ranking problem: models can be significantly skillful in RMSE while dynamically over-smoothed, or dynamically variable while inaccurate.

\subsection{SARIMA and STL+Ridge as diagnostic controls}

SARIMA and STL+Ridge were included to test whether the skill--variance trade-off is specific to direct lag-only ML. SARIMA behaves similarly to HGB and Ridge: it obtains positive median RMSE skill (0.208) but collapses in 110/119 cells (92.4\%). STL+Ridge provides the opposite control case. It avoids variance collapse in all cells and has median $\alpha = 1.399$, but its median RMSE skill is strongly negative ($-1.107$) and all 119 station-horizon cells are significantly worse than persistence under the BH-adjusted DM test. This result shows that preserving amplitude is necessary but not sufficient: the retained variability must also be phase-aligned and forecast-relevant.

\subsection{Sensitivity to the $\alpha$ threshold}

The conclusion is robust to threshold perturbations. At $\alpha < 0.4$, HGB and Ridge still collapse in 92.4\% and 89.9\% of cells, respectively; at $\alpha < 0.5$ both collapse in 99.2\%; and at $\alpha < 0.6$ both collapse in 100.0\%. SARIMA also remains highly collapsed across this range (89.9\%, 92.4\%, and 96.6\%, respectively). Seasonal naive and STL+Ridge remain non-collapsed at these thresholds. The threshold $\alpha = 0.5$ is therefore not driving the qualitative conclusion, although it should be interpreted as a diagnostic convention rather than a universal physical boundary.

\subsection{Diebold--Mariano significance against persistence}

The RMSE gains of the smoothed models are not merely sampling noise. Relative to persistence, the BH-adjusted Diebold--Mariano tests (paired squared losses with the Harvey--Leybourne--Newbold correction \cite{harvey1997}) identify significant improvements in 111/119 HGB cells and 117/119 Ridge cells. SARIMA is significantly better in 44/119 cells. In contrast, STL+Ridge is significantly worse in all 119 cells. These tests make the central paradox explicit: statistically significant RMSE improvements can coexist with severe loss of predictive variance and poor event sensitivity.

\subsection{Operational consequence: exceedance behaviour}

The operational consequence of variance collapse is visible in event detection. For P75 events, mean recall is 26.9\% for HGB, 21.0\% for Ridge, and 15.6\% for SARIMA, compared with 40.0\% for persistence and 32.6\% for seasonal naive. At P90, recall falls to 7.4\%, 5.6\%, and 6.9\%, respectively. STL+Ridge achieves much higher recall (98.4\% at P75 and 87.7\% at P90), but with low precision (mean 24.2\% at P75) and high false-alarm rates. Thus, neither RMSE skill nor variance retention alone is sufficient for operational forecasting: the evaluation must jointly consider amplitude, event detection, and false alarms.

\subsection{Murphy decomposition}

The Murphy decomposition links the empirical findings to forecast verification theory \cite{murphy1988}. The smoothed models reduce error while compressing predictive variability, whereas STL+Ridge preserves or inflates variability but introduces large error components. This decomposition supports the interpretation that the main failure mode is not simple unconditional bias but a mismatch between forecast amplitude, phase, and conditional structure.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Positive skill does not imply dynamic fidelity}
Daily PM$_{10}$ forecasting exhibits a skill--fidelity trade-off: persistence-relative RMSE skill can be positive while point-forecast trajectories retain only a small fraction of observed day-to-day variability. In this study, HGB direct and Ridge direct attain positive RMSE skill in all cells and are significantly better than persistence in most cells under the BH-adjusted DM test; yet, both collapse variance in 118/119 cells (99.2\%) (Table~\ref{tab:five_model_diagnostic_summary}).

\subsection{Variance collapse is not restricted to machine learning}
Including SARIMA shows that variance collapse is not an architectural defect unique to direct ML. SARIMA retains a positive median skill (0.208) but collapses in 110/119 cells (92.4\%), indicating that the skill–fidelity tension can arise in classical statistical baselines as well as in machine-learning models when evaluated against persistence.

\subsection{Variance preservation is not sufficient for usefulness}
Seasonal naive preserves variance (0/119 collapsed cells; median $\alpha \approx 1.000$) but has a slightly negative median skill ($-0.026$) and a high median FAR (0.822). STL+Ridge preserves or inflates variance in all cells (median $\alpha = 1.399$) and shows very high exceedance recall, but its median RMSE skill is strongly negative ($-1.107$) and it exhibits high false-alarm rates (median FAR 0.896). Preserving amplitude is therefore necessary but not sufficient: variability must also be forecast-relevant and not dominated by false alarms.

\subsection{Implications for operational PM$_{10}$ forecasting}
RMSE skill, variance retention, and exceedance behaviour expose different failure modes. Positive baseline-relative RMSE skill can coexist with variance collapse and trajectory smoothing, whereas variance preservation can coexist with weak RMSE skill and high false-alarm rates. Interpreting daily PM$_{10}$ point forecasts therefore requires reading baseline-relative accuracy alongside variance retention and operational exceedance checks, rather than treating any single summary as a universal score.


\section{Implications for Forecast Evaluation Practice}
Minimum reporting practices can substantially improve interpretability in PM forecasting evaluation: (i) report persistence-relative RMSE skill so that gains are anchored to an operational baseline; (ii) report horizon-wise performance rather than only aggregate scores; (iii) disclose temporal validation design and train-only preprocessing boundaries to support deployment-like credibility; and (iv) when point trajectories are interpreted operationally, report variance retention or another trajectory-fidelity diagnostic as a complementary post-evaluation output.

\section{Limitations}

This study is intentionally limited in scope.
The empirical evidence covers 17 MITECO stations at the daily temporal scale;
transfer to other pollutants, temporal resolutions, or national networks is
not assessed.
The five-model comparison is diagnostic rather than exhaustive; the models are intended to reveal failure modes under a common rolling-origin protocol, not to establish a definitive leaderboard.
The model set spans two direct lag-feature ML forecasters (HGB direct, Ridge direct), a sparse classical statistical baseline (SARIMA), a decomposition-based variant (STL+Ridge), and a seasonal naive reference; other architectures, covariate-rich designs, and probabilistic forecasters may exhibit different variance-retention behaviour.
SARIMA is used as a sparse statistical baseline; its results should not be interpreted as the best attainable SARIMA performance under extensive manual specification.
Exceedance diagnostics are included as compact operational checks, not as a full event-classification benchmark.
Skill$_{VP}$ is not proposed as a general-purpose replacement for RMSE or as a
new standard verification score; it is a simple diagnostic auxiliary whose value
lies in making the skill–fidelity decoupling explicit at each horizon.
The collapse threshold $\alpha = 0.5$ is operationally motivated but not derived
from a formal loss criterion. It should be interpreted as a diagnostic convention rather than a universal physical boundary. Threshold-sensitivity results for the five-model experiment (Figure~\ref{fig:threshold_sensitivity}) support the same qualitative conclusion across reasonable perturbations around $\alpha = 0.5$.

\section{Conclusion}

Daily PM$_{10}$ forecasting exhibits a skill--fidelity trade-off under persistence-relative RMSE evaluation. HGB direct, Ridge direct, and SARIMA achieve positive median persistence-relative RMSE skill, but they do so while collapsing forecast variance (median $\alpha$ 0.151, 0.087, and 0.095; collapse rates 99.2\%, 99.2\%, and 92.4\%, respectively). Seasonal naive and STL+Ridge preserve or inflate variance (median $\alpha \approx 1.000$ and 1.399) but do not provide useful RMSE skill on median (median skill $-0.026$ and $-1.107$), and STL+Ridge illustrates that variance preservation can coincide with low precision and high false-alarm rates in exceedance diagnostics (Table~\ref{tab:five_model_diagnostic_summary}).

The central lesson is therefore not that one model family is universally preferable, but that baseline-relative accuracy, variance retention, and exceedance behaviour expose different failure modes. In particular, statistically significant RMSE gains (as confirmed by BH-adjusted Diebold--Mariano tests) can coexist with severe variance collapse and weak event sensitivity, while variance preservation can coexist with poor RMSE skill and high false-alarm rates. Skill$_{VP}$ is retained only as an auxiliary diagnostic to compactly summarise the joint reading of skill and variance retention, not as a universal verification score. A forecast that beats persistence in RMSE is not necessarily dynamically credible, and a forecast that preserves variance is not necessarily operationally useful.
